Direct and Indirect Speech: Complete Rules, Examples & Practice

Narration December 2024 15 min read

Direct and Indirect Speech (also known as Reported Speech) is a fundamental concept in English grammar that allows us to report what someone has said. Understanding the rules for converting direct speech to indirect speech is crucial for effective communication and essential for competitive exams.

What is Direct and Indirect Speech?

Direct Speech

Direct speech reports the exact words spoken by someone, enclosed in quotation marks.

Example: John said, "I am going to the market."

Indirect Speech

Indirect speech reports what someone said without using their exact words, without quotation marks.

Example: John said that he was going to the market.

Basic Rules for Conversion

1. Remove Quotation Marks

Remove the quotation marks and add 'that' after the reporting verb.

Direct: She said, "I love reading."
Indirect: She said that she loved reading.

2. Change the Reporting Verb

The reporting verb may change based on the sentence type.

Statement: said/told
Question: asked/inquired
Command: ordered/commanded

3. Change Tenses

Tenses usually shift backward in time (backshift).

Present → Past
Past → Past Perfect
Future → Conditional

4. Change Pronouns

Pronouns change according to the speaker and listener.

I → he/she
you → I/he/she
we → they

Tense Changes in Indirect Speech

Direct Speech Indirect Speech Example

Future Simple Conditional "I will go" → He said he would go

Pronoun Changes

First Person Pronouns

Change according to the subject of the reporting verb:

  • I → he/she (depending on the speaker's gender)
  • me → him/her
  • my → his/her
  • mine → his/hers
  • we → they
  • us → them
  • our → their
  • ours → theirs

Second Person Pronouns

Change according to the object of the reporting verb:

  • you → I/he/she/they (depending on who is being addressed)
  • your → my/his/her/their
  • yours → mine/his/hers/theirs

Third Person Pronouns

Usually remain unchanged:

  • he, she, it, they (no change)
  • him, her, them (no change)
  • his, her, its, their (no change)

Different Sentence Types

1. Assertive Sentences

Use 'that' after the reporting verb.

Direct: He said, "I am happy."
Indirect: He said that he was happy.

2. Interrogative Sentences

Wh-questions: Use the question word

Direct: She asked, "Where are you going?"
Indirect: She asked where I was going.

Yes/No questions: Use 'if' or 'whether'

Direct: He asked, "Are you coming?"
Indirect: He asked if/whether I was coming.

3. Imperative Sentences

Use 'to' + infinitive for commands/requests

Direct: She said, "Close the door."
Indirect: She told me to close the door.

Use 'not to' + infinitive for negative commands

Direct: He said, "Don't go there."
Indirect: He told me not to go there.

4. Exclamatory Sentences

Use 'exclaimed' and convert to assertive form

Direct: She said, "What a beautiful day!"
Indirect: She exclaimed that it was a very beautiful day.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Mistake 1: Not changing tenses properly

Wrong: He said that he is coming tomorrow.

Correct: He said that he was coming the next day.

❌ Mistake 2: Forgetting to change pronouns

Wrong: She told me, "I will help you" → She told me that I will help you.

Correct: She told me that she would help me.

❌ Mistake 3: Not changing time expressions

Wrong: He said that he would come tomorrow.

Correct: He said that he would come the next day.

❌ Mistake 4: Using wrong reporting verbs

Wrong: He said me that he was tired.

Correct: He told me that he was tired.

Practice Exercises

Exercise 1: Convert to Indirect Speech

  1. Mary said, "I am reading a book."
  2. The teacher asked, "Have you completed your homework?"
  3. John said, "I will visit my grandmother tomorrow."
  4. She said, "Don't make noise."
  5. He exclaimed, "What a wonderful performance!"

Answers:

  1. Mary said that she was reading a book.
  2. The teacher asked if/whether I had completed my homework.
  3. John said that he would visit his grandmother the next day.
  4. She told me not to make noise.
  5. He exclaimed that it was a wonderful performance.

Conclusion

Mastering direct and indirect speech requires understanding the systematic changes in tenses, pronouns, and time expressions. Regular practice with different sentence types will help you become proficient in this important grammar concept. Remember to pay attention to the context and apply the rules consistently.
